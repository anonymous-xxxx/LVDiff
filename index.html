Abstract

Despite significant progress in the field of text-to-video (T2V) diffusion models, the extension of video lengths has been constrained by the limitations in computational resources. Recent studies offer partial remedies to generate longer videos, but fail to achieve satisfactory results in terms of temporal consistency and frame quality. In this paper, we tackle these challenges from a novel viewpoint of integrating high-quality T2I models into T2V models, and propose LVDiff, a training-free and plug-and-play inference framework capable of producing long, scene-consistent and highly aesthetic videos. Technically, an image noise prior is first obtained by adding noise to a visually realistic image generated by T2I. Next, we introduce a Residual Noise Guidance Sampling method that employs a residual-like scheme for noise prediction, yielding high-fidelity videos aligned with the reference image. However, this approach is a double-edged sword leading to limited motion and poor variations in generated videos. To mitigate this, we further introduce a Dynamic Injection Filter that effectively exhibits larger variations by employing spectral modulation in the frequency domain. To improve temporal consistency, we design a novel Global Appearance Consistency mechanism, which establishes long-term connections across short video clips, and leverages a segmentation mask to prevent the model from forgetting the initial scene. Extensive experiments demonstrate the superiority and effectiveness of LVDiff for longer video generation under both single- and multi-text conditions. The code will be released upon the acceptance.

Pipeline

Illustration of the LVDiff framework. LVDiff is a training-free and plug-and-play long video diffusion inference framework that adopts the "noising-then-denoising" strategy. It starts with adding noise to a reference image generated by high-quality T2I models to create an image noise prior. During the denoising stage, Residual Noise Guidance Sampling (RNGS) executes a residual-like scheme for noise prediction, ensuring close alignment with the reference image. To further enhance visual dynamics, the Dynamic Injection Filter (DIF) reinitializes the initial noise by blending the low-frequency components of noise prior with the high-frequency components of Gaussian noise. Additionally, the Global Appearance Consistency (GAC) mechanism maintains long-term dependency and scene consistency across short video clips within a long video, making it robust against the loss of object appearance or scene details during generation.
